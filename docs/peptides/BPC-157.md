# BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound 157)

**Category:** Tissue Repair, Gut Health & Recovery
**Also known as:** Body Protection Compound-157, BPC157, PL-10, PL 14736, Bepecin, Pentadecapeptide BPC 157, Stable Gastric Pentadecapeptide
**Source:** RBT Supplier Catalogue + Tavily Research
**Last updated:** 2026-04-09

---

## Overview

BPC-157 is a synthetic pentadecapeptide — a chain of 15 amino acids — derived from a naturally occurring protein sequence found in human gastric juice. Its full amino acid sequence is: Gly-Glu-Pro-Pro-Pro-Gly-Lys-Pro-Ala-Asp-Asp-Ala-Gly-Leu-Val.

**History:** Research on BPC-157 began in the early 1990s at the University of Zagreb, Croatia, led by Dr. Predrag Sikiric and his group. The peptide was originally isolated from human gastric juice and identified as a naturally occurring cytoprotective compound — hence "Body Protection Compound." It reached Phase II clinical trials for inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) in Europe under the designation PL 14736, though no trial has progressed to Phase III or regulatory approval. Over 100 peer-reviewed studies have been published, the vast majority in animal models. The peptide is notable for its remarkable stability in human gastric juice, unlike most peptides which are degraded by stomach acid — making oral administration a viable (if less potent) delivery route.

**Classification:** Synthetic peptide / Gastric cytoprotective compound / Research chemical

## Mechanism of Action

BPC-157 operates through several intersecting molecular pathways that together produce its tissue-healing, anti-inflammatory, and organ-protective effects:

### 1. VEGFR2-Akt-eNOS Pathway (Angiogenesis)
BPC-157 activates and internalises VEGFR2 (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor Receptor 2) in a mechanism that is independent of VEGF-A itself. This triggers the VEGFR2-Akt-eNOS signalling cascade, stimulating angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation). This is considered its primary mechanism for accelerating tissue repair — new blood vessels restore oxygen and nutrient delivery to damaged tissue. *Source: Hsieh et al., 2016, Journal of Molecular Medicine.*

### 2. EGR-1 Gene Stimulation & Collagen Synthesis
BPC-157 stimulates the expression of EGR-1 (Early Growth Response-1) mRNA in intestinal epithelial cells, along with its co-repressor NAB2. EGR-1 drives production of cytokines, growth factors, and early extracellular matrix (collagen) formation. This explains accelerated collagen deposition and organised wound healing seen in studies.

### 3. Growth Hormone Receptor Upregulation
In tendon fibroblasts, BPC-157 dose-dependently upregulates Growth Hormone Receptor (GHR) expression. This sensitises local tissue to circulating GH, amplifying the anabolic repair response. This is a key mechanism underlying tendon and ligament healing. *Source: PMC6271067.*

### 4. Nitric Oxide (NO) System Modulation
BPC-157 interacts with the nitric oxide system to provide endothelial protection. It counteracts NO-system disruption caused by NSAIDs (e.g., indomethacin), restoring microvascular integrity and mucosal barrier function.

### 5. Tight Junction Reinforcement (Gut Barrier)
BPC-157 directly strengthens intestinal tight junctions — the protein complexes sealing the space between gut epithelial cells. This prevents translocation of luminal antigens and endotoxins into the bloodstream, a central mechanism in "leaky gut" repair.

### 6. Neurotransmitter Modulation
In animal studies, BPC-157 modulates serotonin synthesis in multiple brain regions (substantia nigra, hippocampus, hypothalamus), and interacts with dopaminergic and GABAergic pathways. Acute dosing (10 μg/kg SC) in rats showed increased serotonin in the substantia nigra and decreased synthesis in hippocampus and hypothalamus. These effects shift with chronic dosing. The peptide also appears to interact with the opioid system via dopaminergic pathways for pain modulation.

### 7. Anti-inflammatory Cytokine Suppression
BPC-157 down-regulates pro-inflammatory and pro-cachectic cytokines. In rodent colitis models, it reduces macroscopic inflammation markers, lesion size, and inflammatory cytokine activity.

### 8. Fibroblast Activation & Cell Proliferation
BPC-157 promotes fibroblast proliferation and migration, drives organised collagen deposition, and supports re-epithelialization across multiple tissue types.

## Primary Use Cases

### Clinical / Research Applications
- **Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD):** Most developed clinical application; reached Phase II trials in Europe. Preclinical data on ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease models is robust.
- **Gastric & Duodenal Ulcers:** Extensive rodent data on ethanol-, stress-, and cysteamine-induced ulcers; BPC-157 consistently heals faster than controls.
- **NSAID-Induced GI Damage:** BPC-157 counteracts the GI toxicity of NSAIDs by restoring prostaglandin-like cytoprotection and repairing intestinal permeability.
- **Musculoskeletal Injury Recovery:** Tendon, ligament, and muscle healing across multiple rodent models (Achilles tendon detachment, quadriceps transection, etc.)
- **Short Bowel Syndrome / Fistula Healing:** Animal data shows BPC-157 can heal gastrointestinal fistulas and reverse intestinal resection damage.

### Biohacker / Performance Uses (Anecdotal + Preclinical Basis)
- **Injury recovery acceleration:** The most common self-reported use — tendon and ligament injuries, sports injuries, post-surgery healing
- **Gut healing:** IBS, leaky gut, SIBO-related inflammation, NSAID/antibiotic damage recovery
- **Chronic inflammation:** Joint inflammation, systemic inflammatory conditions
- **Neurological support / mood:** Anecdotally reported anxiolytic and mood-stabilising effects (plausible via gut-brain axis and serotonin/dopamine modulation)
- **Post-workout recovery:** Muscle soreness and microtear repair

## Risks & Contraindications

### Side Effects (from animal studies and limited human data)
- **Injection site reaction** — most common; redness, irritation, typically resolves within hours
- **Nausea, mild dizziness, headache** — rare at standard doses (250–500 mcg/day)
- **Fatigue** — occasionally reported
- **No serious adverse events** reported in 100+ published studies; no organ toxicity, no carcinogenic signals, no hormonal disruption observed

### Theoretical / Precautionary Concerns
- **Cancer risk:** BPC-157 is pro-angiogenic. Theoretical concern that it could promote tumour vascularisation in individuals with existing malignancy. **Contraindicated in active cancer.**
- **Coagulation interactions:** Animal studies show BPC-157 interacts with the coagulation system. **Use with caution if on warfarin, heparin, DOACs, or high-dose aspirin.**
- **Immunogenicity risk:** FDA has flagged potential for immune reactions, particularly with certain administration routes and peptide impurities from poorly characterised API.
- **Long-term safety:** Simply unknown. No long-term human data exists.
- **Contamination risk:** Unregulated sources carry risk of impure or mislabelled product.

### Regulatory Status
- **FDA (USA):** BPC-157 is classified as a **Category 2** bulk drug substance — meaning it presents "significant safety risks" and compounding pharmacies in the US cannot legally compound it for routine use. As of 2025–2026, it remains off the approved compounding list. It is NOT FDA-approved for any indication.
- **WADA:** BPC-157 is **prohibited** for competitive athletes under Section S0 (Non-Approved Substances). WADA classifies it as a substance not approved by any regulatory authority for human therapeutic use.
- **UK / EU / Australia:** Not approved; legal status varies by country; generally treated as an unlicensed research chemical.

## Dosing Reference

*All dosing information is from preclinical literature and practitioner protocols. No established human dosing exists. This is for reference only.*

| Protocol | Dose | Frequency | Duration |
|---------|------|-----------|----------|
| Standard (injury/healing) | 250–500 mcg | Once daily | 4–8 weeks |
| Conservative start | 200 mcg | Once daily | 4 weeks, then reassess |
| Higher-end protocol | 500–1,000 mcg | Once daily | Up to 8 weeks |
| Maintenance (if continued) | 200–250 mcg | Every other day | After initial cycle |

**Administration routes:**
- **Subcutaneous injection** (most common; highest bioavailability for systemic effects)
- **Intramuscular injection** (preferred by some practitioners for localized soft tissue injuries — inject proximate to injury site)
- **Oral (capsules/solution)** — viable due to gastric stability; animal data suggests equivalent efficacy for GI conditions; may be less effective for systemic musculoskeletal applications

**Cycle protocol:** Most practitioners recommend 4–8 weeks on, followed by a break. Long-term continuous use is not supported by evidence.

**Reconstitution:** Typically lyophilised (freeze-dried) powder; reconstituted with bacteriostatic water.
