## VIP (Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide)

**Category:** Immune & Neurological Support  
**Also known as:** Vasoactive Intestinal Polypeptide, VIP neuropeptide, intranasal VIP

---

### Overview & Mechanism

VIP is a naturally occurring 28-amino acid neuropeptide produced throughout the body — in the gut, lung, immune cells, and central nervous system. It mediates its effects through VPAC1 and VPAC2 receptors, which are class B G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs). Key biological actions include:

- **Vasodilation & bronchodilation:** Relaxes smooth muscle in pulmonary vasculature and airways
- **Immunomodulation:** Suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-12), promotes regulatory T-cell differentiation, and limits innate immune overactivation
- **Neuroprotection:** Acts as a neurotrophic factor; found in high concentrations in hypothalamus and limbic system
- **Endocrine/exocrine regulation:** Stimulates pancreatic and intestinal secretion; inhibits gastric acid production
- **Circadian regulation:** Plays a central role in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (master clock)

In CIRS (Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome), biotoxin exposure depletes endogenous VIP levels, contributing to chronic dysregulation of TGF-β1, VEGF, and other inflammatory mediators. Exogenous intranasal VIP is used as a late-stage correction in the Shoemaker protocol.

---

### Primary Use Cases

**Clinical / research:**
- Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (CIRS) — Step 12 of Shoemaker Protocol
- Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) — studied as inhaled therapy
- Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis)
- Autoimmune and chronic inflammatory conditions
- Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS)

**Biohacker / functional medicine:**
- Long COVID / post-viral inflammatory syndromes
- Chronic fatigue with mold/biotoxin history
- Neuroinflammation and cognitive fog
- Sleep and circadian rhythm disruption (VIP is involved in SCN pacemaking)
- Gut motility and IBS support

---

### Evidence Quality

**Moderate — small human studies + animal + mechanistic**

- Dr. Ritchie Shoemaker has published human observational studies showing VIP corrects CIRS biomarkers (TGF-β1, C4a, VEGF) — not RCTs, but structured clinical observations
- RNA-Seq research (Ryan & Shoemaker) demonstrated metabolic and immune shifts in CIRS patients treated with VIP
- Animal and in vitro studies are robust for anti-inflammatory mechanisms
- Pulmonary hypertension: Phase II trial (Petkov et al., 2003, *J Clin Invest*) showed haemodynamic improvement with inhaled VIP — limited by small n
- Overall: meaningful mechanistic and observational data; no large-scale RCTs

---

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ History of mold exposure, water-damaged building illness, or diagnosed CIRS
- ✅ Chronic fatigue, brain fog, and multiple unexplained inflammatory symptoms not responding to standard treatment
- ✅ Elevated TGF-β1, C4a, or depleted MSH on labs
- ✅ Mast cell activation or histamine intolerance symptoms (flushing, gut issues, chemical sensitivities)
- ✅ Chronic respiratory symptoms or diagnosed pulmonary hypertension
- ✅ Post-COVID inflammatory syndrome with ongoing dysautonomia or cognitive symptoms

---

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

**Side effects:**
- Facial flushing and warmth (most common — transient)
- Nausea, lightheadedness shortly after dosing
- Hypotension (especially in dehydrated users)
- Potential tumour growth concern: VIP has been found to stimulate some cancer cell lines *in vitro* — caution in patients with active malignancy

**Contraindications:**
- Active cancer (in vitro evidence of tumour-growth stimulation)
- Hypotension or vasodilatory conditions
- Should only be used as last step in CIRS protocol after prior steps completed — premature use can worsen outcomes

**Regulatory:**
- Not FDA-approved; available as compounded intranasal spray through licensed compounding pharmacies (prescription required in the US)
- The FDA has reviewed compounded VIP and it remains on the market as a compounded preparation — ISEAI advocacy community has pushed for continued access
- **Not on the WADA prohibited list**
- Outside the US, regulatory status varies — generally unregulated but not approved

---

### Education Hook Facts

- 💡 VIP was first discovered in pig intestines in 1970 — researchers didn't know it was also a master regulator of the immune system and brain clock
- 💡 People with mold illness often have VIP levels near zero — their body's anti-inflammatory "dimmer switch" is effectively turned off
- 💡 VIP is so potent at suppressing inflammation that it's been explored as a treatment for sepsis and rheumatoid arthritis

---

### Dosing Reference

**Standard CIRS / Shoemaker Protocol:**
- 50 mcg (1 spray) intranasally, 4× daily (alternate nostrils)
- Typical cycle: 1 month minimum; reassess inflammatory markers
- Only begin after ERMI-safe environment confirmed and prior protocol steps completed

**General compounded intranasal:**
- Inflammatory support: 0.2–0.4 mg/day divided doses
- Bottles typically contain 120 sprays (12 mL), providing ~30-day supply at 4× daily dosing

**Route:** Intranasal spray (primary); subcutaneous injection (research/less common)  
**Reconstitution:** Pre-compounded solution — no reconstitution required in clinical setting

## Bronchogen

**Category:** Respiratory & Lung Support  
**Also known as:** AEDL tetrapeptide, Khavinson lung bioregulator

---

### Overview & Mechanism

Bronchogen is a synthetic tetrapeptide (Ala-Glu-Asp-Leu / AEDL) developed through Professor Vladimir Khavinson's bioregulator research programme at the St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology. Like all Khavinson bioregulators, it is an ultra-short peptide designed to be tissue-specific — in this case, targeting bronchial and pulmonary epithelial tissue.

**Proposed mechanisms:**
- **Epigenetic gene regulation:** Bronchogen is proposed to penetrate cell and nuclear membranes, bind to DNA at CNG sites (cytosine methylation targets), and modulate transcription of lung-specific genes
- **Lung cell gene targets:** Activates NKX2-1, SCGB1A1, SCGB3A2, FOXA1, and FOXA2 — all markers of bronchial epithelial differentiation and identity
- **Anti-inflammatory:** Attenuates inflammatory mediator production in bronchial epithelial cell models; shown to reduce fibrosis markers in murine bleomycin-induced lung injury models
- **Surfactant regulation:** Research suggests influence on surfactant production, relevant to alveolar function
- **Cellular senescence:** May counteract age-related decline in lung epithelial cell gene expression patterns

---

### Primary Use Cases

**Research / functional medicine:**
- Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) — lung tissue support
- Pulmonary fibrosis — anti-fibrotic potential in animal models
- Asthma — bronchial epithelial cell integrity
- Age-related lung function decline
- Post-COVID pulmonary damage recovery (community use, not studied)

**Biohacker use:**
- Paired with other Khavinson bioregulators in anti-aging "stack" protocols
- Smokers seeking respiratory tissue restoration
- Athletes with impaired lung function or altitude adaptation goals

---

### Evidence Quality

**Weak — predominantly in vitro and animal studies; no human RCTs**

- Research from Khavinson's group demonstrates DNA-binding activity and gene expression changes in bronchial epithelial cell cultures
- Animal data (murine bleomycin model) supports anti-fibrotic effects
- No published randomised controlled trials in humans
- All human-relevant evidence is extrapolated from cell culture and rodent studies
- Published primarily in Russian-language journals and the St. Petersburg institute's own publications — limited independent replication
- Community use is widespread in biohacker circles, but anecdotal

---

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ History of smoking, occupational lung exposure, or diagnosed COPD/chronic bronchitis
- ✅ Pulmonary fibrosis or post-COVID lung damage seeking tissue-level support
- ✅ Asthma with interest in epithelial repair beyond symptom management
- ✅ Longevity-focused user running Khavinson bioregulator stack targeting organ-specific aging
- ✅ Reduced exercise capacity or VO2 max linked to respiratory limitation
- ✅ Interest in epigenetic approaches to lung tissue maintenance

---

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

**Side effects:**
- No significant adverse effects reported in available research
- Injection site reactions possible with subcutaneous administration
- Long-term safety data in humans is absent — unknown risk profile

**Contraindications:**
- No established contraindications; use with caution in active lung malignancy (insufficient data)
- Not suitable for acute respiratory emergencies — not a bronchodilator

**Regulatory:**
- **Not FDA-approved** for any indication
- Sold as "research chemical" or "research peptide" in most markets
- Not on WADA prohibited list
- Available through peptide suppliers in the US, EU, and globally without prescription — regulatory grey area
- Some natural extract versions (Taxorest) sold as supplements in Russia/Eastern Europe

---

### Education Hook Facts

- 💡 Bronchogen's four-amino acid sequence (AEDL) is so short it may pass directly into cell nuclei and interact with DNA — a mechanism almost unheard of in conventional pharmacology
- 💡 Unlike bronchodilators that only open airways temporarily, Bronchogen is theorised to address the underlying gene expression changes that cause respiratory tissue to age and degrade
- 💡 Khavinson's bioregulator programme originated from Soviet military research into how to maintain peak performance in soldiers and cosmonauts under extreme physiological stress

---

### Dosing Reference

**Community / research protocols:**

| Protocol | Dose | Frequency | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard anti-aging | 500 mcg/day | Once daily (SC, evening) | 10–30 days |
| Chronic respiratory disease | 10 mg/day | Once daily (SC) | 30 days on / 2–4 months off |
| Oral (capsule form) | 20 mg/day | Divided doses | 30 days |

**Notes:**
- Subcutaneous injection is preferred for research-grade peptide (lyophilised, requires reconstitution with bacteriostatic water)
- Oral bioavailability is debated — Khavinson's team claims the peptides survive GI transit due to their small size; independent confirmation is lacking
- Typical cycle: 30 days on, then 3–6 months off before repeat

## Cardiogen

**Category:** Cardiovascular & Cardiac Support  
**Also known as:** AEDR tetrapeptide, Khavinson cardiac bioregulator, Ala-Glu-Asp-Arg

---

### Overview & Mechanism

Cardiogen is a synthetic tetrapeptide (Ala-Glu-Asp-Arg / AEDR) from the Khavinson bioregulator family, specifically targeting cardiac tissue. It is designed to be heart-selective, meaning its gene expression modulation effects are proposed to act preferentially on cardiomyocytes and cardiac fibroblasts.

**Proposed mechanisms:**
- **Cardiomyocyte proliferation:** Promotes growth of cardiac muscle cells while inhibiting fibroblast overgrowth — potentially reducing cardiac fibrosis
- **Anti-apoptotic in cardiac cells:** Downregulates p53 expression in cardiomyocytes, reducing programmed cell death after injury
- **Mitochondrial support:** Enhances mitochondrial efficiency and antioxidant enzyme activity (superoxide dismutase, glutathione peroxidase) in heart tissue
- **Anti-inflammatory:** Reduces TNF-α and IL-1β levels in cardiac tissue post-injury
- **Telomerase pathway:** Some Khavinson bioregulator protocols report telomere length improvements with combined stacks — Cardiogen's specific contribution is unclear but is included in longevity stacks for this purpose
- **Lifespan extension (animal data):** Chronic Cardiogen in aged animals reportedly extended lifespan by 20–30% and improved systemic markers beyond the heart

---

### Primary Use Cases

**Research / clinical interest:**
- Cardiac tissue regeneration post-MI (myocardial infarction)
- Chronic heart failure — reducing maladaptive remodelling
- Age-related cardiac decline (cardiomyocyte loss, increased fibrosis)
- Post-surgical cardiac recovery support

**Biohacker / longevity use:**
- Core component of Khavinson anti-aging bioregulator stacks
- Paired with Ventfort (vascular bioregulator) for cardiovascular longevity
- Athletes seeking cardiac resilience and mitochondrial efficiency

---

### Evidence Quality

**Weak — in vitro and animal studies only; no human RCTs**

- Cell culture studies from Khavinson group show cardiomyocyte proliferation effects and reduced apoptosis via p53 downregulation
- Animal studies (rat models including M-1 sarcoma model) show differential pro-apoptotic effects on tumour cells vs. anti-apoptotic in cardiac cells — interesting but mechanistically not fully explained
- Lifespan extension data (20–30%) from aged animal cohorts — published in Russian-language biogerontology journals; limited independent replication
- No human clinical trials published
- Broader Khavinson bioregulator clinical study (Lawrence, 2021) reported telomere age improvements in a 124-person cohort using combined bioregulator stacks — but methodology has significant limitations (no control group, multiple interventions simultaneously)

---

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ History of heart attack, cardiac event, or diagnosed heart failure
- ✅ Strong family history of cardiovascular disease with proactive longevity interest
- ✅ Age 50+ with concern about cardiac aging and declining exercise tolerance
- ✅ Running a Khavinson bioregulator stack and seeking heart-specific coverage
- ✅ Post-COVID myocarditis or cardiac involvement recovery
- ✅ Elevated cardiac inflammation markers or echo showing early remodelling changes

---

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

**Side effects:**
- No significant adverse effects established in available literature
- Injection site reactions with subcutaneous use
- Long-term safety data in humans is absent
- The p53 downregulation mechanism (anti-apoptotic) theoretically raises caution in individuals with active or latent malignancy — though the same research shows pro-apoptotic effects in tumour tissue (the mechanism is cell-type dependent)

**Contraindications:**
- Active malignancy: caution given p53 modulation (theoretical concern)
- Should not replace standard cardiac medications; no interaction data available

**Regulatory:**
- **Not FDA-approved** for any indication
- Sold as "research peptide" globally — no prescription required in most markets
- Not on WADA prohibited list
- In Russia, Khavinson's work has more institutional recognition; some natural organ-extract versions (Chelohart) are available as OTC supplements

---

### Education Hook Facts

- 💡 The heart loses roughly 1 billion cardiomyocytes per decade after age 40 — Cardiogen is one of the only compounds studied for its potential to stimulate new cardiac muscle cell growth
- 💡 In aged rat studies, Cardiogen didn't just improve heart function — it appeared to extend total lifespan by up to 30%, suggesting systemic benefits beyond the heart
- 💡 Unlike most heart drugs that manage symptoms, Cardiogen is studied at the gene expression level — attempting to tell aging heart cells to behave like younger ones

---

### Dosing Reference

**Community / research protocols:**

| Protocol | Dose | Frequency | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard anti-aging | 500 mcg/day | Once daily (SC, evening) | 10–30 days |
| Post-cardiac event support | 1–2 mg/day | Once daily (SC) | 30 days |
| Oral (capsule) | 20 mg/day | Divided | 30 days |

**Cycle:** Typically 10–30 days on, then 3–6 months off before repeat  
**Stacking:** Commonly paired with Ventfort (vessel bioregulator) and Endoluten (pineal/longevity bioregulator)  
**Reconstitution:** Lyophilised powder; reconstitute with bacteriostatic water for injection

## GcMAF (Gc-Macrophage Activating Factor)

**Category:** Immune Modulation  
**Also known as:** Gc protein-derived macrophage activating factor, Vitamin D-binding protein MAF, GcMAF immunotherapy

---

### Overview & Mechanism

GcMAF is a protein derived from the vitamin D-binding protein (Gc protein / group-specific component). In normal physiology, Gc protein is converted to GcMAF through sequential deglycosylation by β-galactosidase (from B cells) and sialidase (from T cells). GcMAF then activates macrophages — white blood cells that are central to immune surveillance and tumour destruction.

**Biological pathway:**
- Gc protein → enzymatic modification → GcMAF
- GcMAF binds to macrophage cell surfaces → triggers macrophage activation → activated macrophages become potently cytotoxic against tumour cells and pathogens
- **Nagalase connection:** The enzyme nagalase (α-N-acetylgalactosaminidase) — produced by cancer cells, viruses (HIV, hepatitis), and some bacteria — deglycosylates Gc protein, preventing GcMAF formation and thereby suppressing macrophage activation. Elevated nagalase is proposed as a marker of immune suppression
- Proponents argue that exogenous GcMAF bypasses the nagalase block, restoring macrophage function

**Key claim (highly contested):** Yamamoto et al. published papers (later retracted) claiming 100% remission rates in breast, colorectal, and prostate cancer with GcMAF immunotherapy — these results have never been independently replicated.

---

### Primary Use Cases

**Claimed / researched (not validated):**
- Cancer immunotherapy (breast, colorectal, prostate — retracted studies)
- HIV/AIDS immune support
- Autism (claimed nagalase elevation in autism — scientifically disputed)
- Chronic viral infections

**Why people seek it:**
- Desperate cancer patients seeking alternatives to conventional treatment
- Practitioners promoting anti-nagalase protocols
- Biohackers interested in macrophage immune priming

---

### Evidence Quality

**Very weak — retracted studies, serious scientific controversy**

- The foundational Yamamoto studies (breast cancer, colorectal cancer, prostate cancer) — published 2008–2009 — were **retracted** in 2014 due to "irregularities in documentation for institutional review board approval"
- Other researchers have failed to replicate Yamamoto's results and have found no consistent differences in GcMAF levels between cancer patients and healthy controls
- Some legitimate in vitro and animal research exists showing GcMAF's biological activity (macrophage activation is real and well-characterised as a normal physiological process)
- The nagalase-autism link is scientifically unsupported and has been used to exploit vulnerable families
- A small number of non-retracted studies suggest some anti-tumour activity in cell culture — but this is a very long way from clinical efficacy
- **Honest summary:** The biological mechanism (GcMAF activates macrophages) is real. The therapeutic claims are not substantiated by credible human evidence.

---

### Quiz Signals

> ⚠️ **Note for quiz design:** GcMAF should be approached with significant caution in any recommendation engine. It is not appropriate to recommend GcMAF as a cancer treatment. Inclusion in a quiz should focus on immune support interest and education — not therapeutic claims.

- ✅ Interest in macrophage-based immune priming or innate immune optimisation
- ✅ Elevated nagalase on specialised functional medicine testing
- ✅ History of chronic viral infection with ongoing immune suppression
- ✅ User researching immune oncology alongside conventional treatment (NOT as a replacement)
- ⚠️ Active cancer patients — direct firmly toward oncologist; flag regulatory risks
- ⚠️ Autism parents seeking nagalase treatment — this use is unsupported and historically exploitative

---

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

**Safety concerns:**
- No standardised manufacturing process — product purity and dose are unknown in non-clinical settings
- Risk of immune over-activation or cytokine storm
- Unknown long-term effects
- Blood product origin (some formulations are derived from blood) — infection transmission risk

**Regulatory — HIGHLY CONCERNING:**
- **UK:** MHRA raided and seized over 10,000 vials from an unlicensed production facility in Cambridgeshire (Milton); production shut down. David Noakes, who ran GcMAF operations via Guernsey and Switzerland, was **criminally prosecuted** and jailed for selling unlicensed medicine
- **Switzerland:** Authorities investigated and shut down Lausanne GcMAF clinic
- **US:** No FDA approval; selling as a therapeutic product is illegal. Several associated US practitioners faced legal and licensing consequences
- **EU:** No EMA approval; unlicensed in all member states
- **Global:** Considered an unlicensed medicine requiring marketing authorisation virtually everywhere
- **Not on WADA prohibited list** — but sports relevance is negligible

**⚠️ Commercial risk:** Selling or marketing GcMAF as a treatment — for any indication — carries **high legal risk** in the UK, US, and EU. This is not a viable product for a legitimate peptide business without extraordinary legal groundwork.

---

### Education Hook Facts

- 💡 GcMAF is not a foreign chemical — it's a protein your body makes naturally every day as part of normal immune surveillance. The controversy is about whether injecting more of it actually works as therapy
- 💡 The nagalase theory is compelling in concept: cancer cells hijack your immune system by disabling the very protein that would activate macrophages to kill them. The science behind this mechanism is real — the clinical translation is not yet proven
- 💡 The GcMAF story is also a cautionary tale: the original researcher's papers were retracted, a key US proponent died under mysterious circumstances, and operators were criminally jailed — yet it still circulates as a "suppressed cure"

---

### Dosing Reference

> ⚠️ Providing dosing information for an unregulated, criminally prosecuted product is not appropriate for a commercial platform. The following is included for educational/research context only.

**From historical research context (Yamamoto protocol — studies retracted):**
- 100 ng intramuscular injection, once weekly
- Administered over several months
- Nagalase testing used to monitor response

**Current community use:** Various claimed preparations including subcutaneous injection and oral "colostrum-derived" GcMAF — none standardised, none validated for content or purity.

**Recommendation:** GcMAF should not be stocked or recommended by a legitimate peptide company without specific legal counsel. Educate users about the biology; do not position as a therapy.

## PT-141 (Bremelanotide)

**Category:** Sexual Health & Libido  
**Also known as:** Bremelanotide, Vyleesi (brand name), melanocortin agonist

---

### Overview & Mechanism

PT-141 (bremelanotide) is a synthetic cyclic heptapeptide analogue of α-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (α-MSH). It was originally developed as a tanning agent (the "Barbie drug" Melanotan II is a close relative) before researchers noticed it reliably induced sexual arousal — famously causing an unintended prolonged erection in a researcher who self-administered it.

**Mechanism — centrally mediated, unlike Viagra:**
- Activates melanocortin receptors **MC3R and MC4R** in the hypothalamus and limbic system
- These receptors modulate pathways governing sexual desire, arousal, and motivation through CNS signalling
- **Does not work via peripheral vasodilation** — it doesn't increase genital blood flow directly; it works on the brain's desire circuitry
- Also has minor activity at MC1R (pigmentation) and MC5R

This central mechanism means it can work in people with psychogenic and organic sexual dysfunction where PDE5 inhibitors (Viagra, Cialis) fail — because those drugs require intact vascular response.

**FDA approval status:** Approved June 2019 as **Vyleesi** for **Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder (HSDD) in premenopausal women**. First FDA-approved on-demand treatment for female sexual desire disorder.

---

### Primary Use Cases

**Approved (FDA):**
- HSDD (Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder) in premenopausal women — low sexual desire causing personal distress not explained by relationship issues or other medical conditions

**Off-label / research:**
- Male erectile dysfunction — particularly in men who don't respond to PDE5 inhibitors
- Male low libido / hypoactive sexual desire
- Female sexual arousal disorder (FSAD)
- Men and women with psychogenic sexual dysfunction (anxiety, trauma-related)

**Biohacker use:**
- On-demand libido enhancement (men and women)
- Combined with PDE5 inhibitors for synergistic effect in men (dual mechanism: central desire + peripheral vasodilation)

---

### Evidence Quality

**Strong for approved indication; moderate for off-label male use**

**Women (HSDD):**
- Multiple Phase 2 and Phase 3 RCTs supporting FDA approval
- Demonstrated improvement in desire scores and reduction in sexual distress vs. placebo
- Effect sizes moderate — not all women respond; works best in those with no other cause for low desire
- Approved dose 1.75 mg SC showed ~40% nausea rate (first dose), ~20% flushing

**Men (off-label):**
- Phase 2A trial (intranasal, 20 mg dose): demonstrated improved erectile function in men with ED, including non-responders to sildenafil
- Phase 2b data: dose-finding in men — clinically meaningful responses seen
- PMC review (2020) confirms evidence base for male use, though FDA approval not pursued for this indication
- No large RCTs in men at approved SC dose; extrapolation from female trials and earlier male studies

---

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ Low sexual desire or difficulty becoming aroused — not explained by relationship problems or medication side effects
- ✅ Erectile dysfunction that hasn't responded well to Viagra or Cialis (PDE5 inhibitor non-responder)
- ✅ Female sexual dysfunction with primary complaint of low desire rather than physical arousal difficulty
- ✅ Stress, anxiety, or psychological factors that suppress libido
- ✅ Post-menopause or post-andropause with loss of sexual interest
- ✅ User wants an on-demand solution (not daily medication) that works at the level of desire, not just physical response

---

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

**Side effects (well-characterised from RCTs):**
- **Nausea:** ~40% with first dose (most common); reduces with subsequent doses; ~13% require antiemetics; lasts ~2 hours
- **Flushing/hot flush:** ~20%
- **Headache:** ~11%
- **Injection site reactions:** ~13%
- **Transient blood pressure increase + heart rate decrease:** Occurs shortly after injection; resolves within 12 hours — significant concern in cardiovascular patients
- **Focal hyperpigmentation:** Darkening of face, gums, breasts with frequent use (>8/month) — more pronounced in darker skin tones
- **Nausea/vomiting:** ~5%

**Contraindications:**
- Cardiovascular disease / unstable hypertension — BP elevation risk
- Concurrent use with nitrates or other antihypertensives (risk of hypotension)
- Not for use in men as FDA-approved indication (off-label only)
- Pregnancy / breastfeeding

**Dosing limits:**
- Max 1 dose per 24 hours
- Max 8 doses per month (approved labelling)

**⚠️ CRITICAL Regulatory Warning — HIGH RISK AS RESEARCH CHEMICAL:**
- PT-141 / bremelanotide is an **FDA-approved prescription drug** (Vyleesi, Palatin Technologies)
- **Selling it as a "research chemical" or "research peptide" in the US is extremely high legal risk** — the FDA has clear jurisdiction over approved drugs sold for human use regardless of labelling
- In the UK and EU, it does not hold marketing authorisation — selling as a therapeutic product requires authorisation; research chemical loophole is increasingly scrutinised
- Compounding pharmacies in the US can legally compound PT-141 with a valid prescription
- **WADA status:** Not on the WADA prohibited list (not a performance-enhancing substance in sports context)
- **Recommendation for any commercial operation:** Consult legal counsel before stocking; at minimum, do not make any therapeutic claims

---

### Education Hook Facts

- 💡 PT-141 was accidentally discovered when a researcher synthesised what he thought was a tanning peptide, self-injected it, and experienced an 8-hour erection — arguably the most consequential lab accident in peptide history
- 💡 Unlike Viagra (which requires you to *want* sex and just helps the mechanics), PT-141 actually *creates* the desire — it works in the part of the brain that generates sexual motivation, not in the genitals
- 💡 PT-141 is the first and only FDA-approved on-demand treatment for low sexual desire in women — a condition that affects an estimated 10% of premenopausal women globally

---

### Dosing Reference

**FDA-approved (women, HSDD):**
- 1.75 mg subcutaneous injection via autoinjector
- Administered at least 45 minutes before anticipated sexual activity
- Maximum: 1 dose per 24 hours; 8 doses per month

**Off-label / compounded (men & women, clinical practice):**

| Route | Typical Dose | Onset | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| SC injection | 0.5–2 mg | 45–90 min | Closest to approved; titrate from 0.5 mg to reduce nausea |
| Intranasal (compounded) | 1–2 mg (0.5–2 mg/spray) | 30–60 min | Faster onset; less nausea reported; not FDA-approved route |

**Practical notes:**
- Starting dose of 0.5–1 mg SC reduces nausea burden; titrate up over subsequent uses
- Ondansetron (4–8 mg) can be taken 30 min before injection to preempt nausea
- Take on relatively empty stomach to reduce nausea
- Men: off-label range typically 1–2 mg SC; Phase 2 research used doses up to 20 mg intranasally but this reflects older trial design, not standard practice
