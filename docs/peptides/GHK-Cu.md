# GHK-Cu (Copper Peptide)

**Category:** Skin Health, Anti-Aging & Tissue Regeneration
**Also known as:** Copper Peptide, GHK:Cu, Glycyl-L-Histidyl-L-Lysine Copper, Copper Tripeptide-1
**Source:** RBT Supplier Catalogue + Tavily Research
**Last updated:** 2026-04-09

---

## Overview

GHK-Cu (glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine:copper) is a naturally occurring copper-binding tripeptide first isolated from human plasma albumin in 1973 by biochemist Dr. Loren Pickart. The peptide is found in human plasma, saliva, and urine and plays an endogenous role in tissue repair and cellular maintenance.

Plasma concentrations of GHK-Cu are approximately **200 ng/mL in young adults** but decline significantly with age — by age 60, levels can drop by 60-70%. This age-related decline correlates with impaired wound healing, skin thinning, and increased inflammation, forming the rationale for supplementation.

Over 50 years of research (predominantly in vitro and animal studies, with some human topical trials) has established GHK-Cu as one of the most extensively studied small peptides in regenerative medicine. It is classified as a **matrikine** — a biologically active peptide fragment derived from extracellular matrix proteins — and acts as a master regulator of wound healing and tissue remodelling signals.

## Mechanism of Action

GHK-Cu exerts its effects through multiple overlapping mechanisms at the cellular and molecular level:

### 1. Gene Modulation (Master Regulator)
The most striking finding from modern gene profiling research is that GHK-Cu appears to regulate a remarkable proportion of the human genome. Studies using the Connectivity Map database found GHK influences the expression of **31–47% of genes** analysed in human cells — affecting pathways controlling cell differentiation, apoptosis, DNA repair, and stem cell function. It can both upregulate protective genes and downregulate genes associated with inflammation and disease progression.

### 2. Collagen & Extracellular Matrix Synthesis
GHK-Cu stimulates fibroblasts to increase production of:
- **Type I and Type III collagen**
- **Elastin** (skin elasticity)
- **Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)** including hyaluronic acid and dermatan sulfate
- **Decorin** (collagen cross-linking and organisation)

This drives structural skin remodelling and improves wound tensile strength.

### 3. Anti-inflammatory Signalling
GHK-Cu suppresses pro-inflammatory pathways including:
- **NF-κB signalling** — blocks expression of inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6)
- **p38 MAPK signalling** — dampens stress-response inflammation
- **TGF-β1 modulation** — reduces fibrous scar formation while promoting healthy remodelling

### 4. Antioxidant Activity
GHK-Cu upregulates antioxidant enzyme systems (superoxide dismutase, catalase) and protects cells from lipid peroxidation by-products. Its copper-binding capacity also plays a role in managing free radical damage.

### 5. Angiogenesis & Wound Healing
GHK-Cu promotes new blood vessel formation (angiogenesis) — critical for delivering nutrients and oxygen to healing tissue. It also activates macrophages, increases fibronectin synthesis, and accelerates re-epithelialisation.

### 6. Proteasome Activation (Cell Cleansing)
Gene profiling shows GHK upregulates genes in the ubiquitin/proteasome system — the cell's primary protein recycling and quality-control machinery. This is believed to reduce accumulation of damaged proteins associated with cellular ageing.

### 7. Epigenetic Effects
Animal studies suggest GHK may modulate histone deacetylase 2 (HDAC2) — an epigenetic regulator linked to inflammation and cognitive decline. This positions it as a potential neurological support compound, though human data is lacking.

## Primary Use Cases

### Clinical / Research Applications
- **Wound healing acceleration** — promoted by many wound care protocols, particularly post-surgical or burn healing
- **Skin repair** — clinical studies show improvements in wrinkle depth, skin elasticity, and skin density with topical GHK-Cu formulations
- **Hair restoration** — stimulates hair follicle proliferation and increases hair follicle size and density; used in topical scalp formulas
- **COPD / Lung repair** — research shows GHK-Cu can restore chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) fibroblasts toward a healthier phenotype
- **Anti-cancer support** — preclinical data suggests GHK-Cu may suppress tumour metastasis genes, though this requires significant further study

### Biohacker / Performance Uses
- **Skin-glow protocol** — the defining use case; typically subcutaneous injection or high-concentration topical serum
- **Stacked with BPC-157 and TB-500** for injury healing and tissue repair
- **Scalp microneedling + GHK-Cu serum** for hair regrowth in men and women
- **Anti-aging stack** alongside NAD+ and growth hormone peptides

## Evidence Quality

**Overall rating: Strong preclinical; Moderate clinical (topical); Limited clinical (injectable)**

| Study Type | Volume | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| In vitro (cell studies) | Extensive (50+ years) | High mechanistic confidence |
| Animal studies | Extensive | Consistent with human mechanisms |
| Human topical RCTs | Moderate (several facial studies) | Positive signals; small samples |
| Human injectable RCTs | Minimal | Mostly anecdotal + clinical reports |

### Notable Research
- **Pickart et al. (1973)** — Original isolation of GHK from human plasma albumin; first observations of tissue regeneration promotion
- **Pickart & Margolina (2018)** — *International Journal of Molecular Sciences* — comprehensive review of GHK-Cu's regenerative and protective actions in human skin; detailed gene profiling data showing effects on 31-47% of human genes studied
- **Dou et al. (2022)** — *Aging Pathobiology and Therapeutics* (PMC 35083444) — Review confirming anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anti-aging properties; NF-κB and p38 MAPK suppression in LPS-induced models
- **Multiple facial cream studies** — Randomised controlled studies (various researchers) showed GHK-Cu containing creams reduced mottled pigmentation, wrinkle depth, and improved skin density vs. control; specific authors include Abdulghani et al. and Leyden et al.
- **Lidsen Geriatrics (Pickart 2018)** — Stem cell gene profiling: GHK-Cu regulation of genes controlling cell differentiation, apoptosis, and stem cell function

**Gaps:** Most injectable/systemic data is from animal models. Human injectable RCTs are lacking. The "copper uglies" phenomenon (anecdotal reports of skin worsening) has no formal study behind it.

## Risks & Contraindications

### Side Effects
- **Generally very well tolerated** — described in research as "safe, inexpensive, and extensively studied"
- **Topical:** Occasional skin irritation, redness, or temporary purging in sensitive skin. The rare "copper uglies" phenomenon — anecdotal reports of accelerated skin aging with overuse — is poorly understood but worth monitoring
- **Injectable:** Injection site redness, bruising, swelling, temporary nausea, mild dizziness (rare)
- **Systemic toxicity:** Very low at therapeutic doses; GHK-Cu is active at nanomolar concentrations
- **Copper overload:** Theoretically possible with very high, prolonged injectable doses; practically very unlikely at standard dosing

### Contraindications
- Active malignancy — GHK-Cu's angiogenic activity is generally considered a caution in cancer contexts (consult physician)
- Wilson's disease (inherited copper metabolism disorder) — absolute contraindication
- Known copper allergy or sensitivity

### Regulatory Status
- **Topical:** Widely available OTC as cosmeceutical ingredient; no prescription required
- **Injectable:** Research peptide in most markets; requires compounding pharmacy or clinical prescription in UK/US; available via private clinics
- **WADA status:** Not currently listed on the WADA Prohibited List (as of 2026); not considered a performance-enhancing drug in sport contexts
- **FDA:** Not FDA-approved as a pharmaceutical drug; off-label injectable use; topical cosmetic use broadly accepted

## Education Hook Facts

1. **GHK-Cu was discovered because it makes old livers act young.** In 1973, Dr. Pickart noticed that old human plasma contained something that caused aged liver cells to behave like young cells in culture — that something turned out to be GHK-Cu. It was literally isolated by looking for the signal of biological youth.

2. **It may affect nearly half your genome.** Gene profiling research suggests GHK-Cu influences the expression of 31–47% of genes studied in human cells — making it one of the broadest-acting naturally occurring small peptides known to science.

3. **Your body already makes it — you just make less as you age.** GHK-Cu is naturally present in human plasma at ~200 ng/mL in youth, but by age 60, levels can fall by 60–70%. The peptide is also found in your saliva and urine.

4. **It can suppress genes linked to cancer metastasis.** Preclinical studies have shown GHK-Cu downregulates genes associated with tumour spread — including genes in the TGF-β pathway that drive metastatic signalling. This is early-stage research, but it points to GHK-Cu's role beyond cosmetics.

5. **It works at nanomolar concentrations.** Unlike many compounds that require large doses, GHK-Cu is biologically active at extremely low levels — comparable to the natural concentrations your body produces. This means small amounts go a long way.

## Dosing Reference

### Topical (Most Common / Entry-Level)
| Goal | Concentration | Frequency | Protocol Length |
|---|---|---|---|
| General anti-aging / skin maintenance | 0.5–1% serum | Once daily (PM) | Ongoing; 3–6 months minimum |
| Active skin repair / post-procedure | 1–2% serum | 1–2x daily | 8–12 weeks |
| Scalp / hair growth | 1% spray or serum | Daily, post-microneedling | 3–6 months |

**Formulation note:** Topical GHK-Cu penetrates best at pH 6–7. Avoid mixing with vitamin C or AHAs in same session.

### Injectable (Subcutaneous)
| Goal | Dose | Frequency | Protocol Length |
|---|---|---|---|
| Systemic anti-aging / skin rejuvenation | 1–2 mg | Daily or 5x/week | 8–12 week cycle |
| Wound healing / recovery stack | 1–2 mg | Daily | Until healed or 4–6 weeks |

**Administration:** Subcutaneous injection, typically abdomen, thigh, or near the target tissue.

**Cycling:** Common protocol: 8–12 weeks on, 4 weeks off. Some users cycle 5 days on / 2 days off weekly.

**Note:** Oral GHK-Cu has poor bioavailability due to peptide degradation in the GI tract. Injectable or topical routes are preferred.
