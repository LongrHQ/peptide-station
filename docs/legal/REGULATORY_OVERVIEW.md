## Executive Summary

The regulatory landscape for consumer peptide sales is **complex, jurisdiction-specific, and evolving rapidly**. The core model — selling peptides as "research chemicals" with a "not for human consumption" disclaimer — is legally tenuous but widely used. In the US, the FDA has become significantly more aggressive, issuing 50+ warning letters in September 2025 alone.

**Key conclusion for Peptide Station:**
- US launch requires dedicated regulatory strategy and specialist counsel from day one
- The "research use only" framing is necessary but insufficient on its own — marketing must be rigorously reviewed
- UK expansion (Phase 2) is comparatively easier under the MHRA framework
- Legal counsel and ongoing regulatory monitoring are non-negotiable

## US Regulatory Landscape (Primary Framework)

> Peptide Station is US-first. This section governs the primary operating market.

### FDA Framework

**Key legislation:** Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA)

**FDA's Position on Peptides:**
- FDA regulates substances as drugs if they are intended for human use and make health claims
- Most consumer peptides are NOT FDA-approved drugs
- FDA has classified several peptides as **"Bulk Drug Substances" that cannot be compounded** under Sections 503A and 503B
- The FDA's enforcement posture has sharply escalated: **50+ warning letters issued in September 2025 alone** targeting peptide vendors, compounders, and manufacturers

### 503A vs 503B Compounding — Key Distinction

**503A Compounding Pharmacies:**
- Compound on a per-patient prescription basis only
- Must use USP-monographed ingredients or FDA-approved bulk substances
- Peptides without monographs (most consumer peptides) cannot be compounded without specific FDA review
- State boards of pharmacy oversight; require valid patient prescription for each compound

**503B Outsourcing Facilities:**
- Can compound for office use (not per-patient prescription)
- Must only compound drugs on the Category 1 bulk list (FDA-approved or with sufficient historical compounding evidence)
- Cannot compound BPC-157, Thymosin Alpha-1, or other Category 2 substances
- Subject to FDA cGMP inspections

**Practical implication for Peptide Station:** The compounding pathway is largely unavailable for core products (BPC-157 is Category 2; CJC-1295/Ipamorelin under review). The research-use-only model is the operative framework for launch.

### FDA Peptide Classifications (Updated January 2025)

**BPC-157: Category 2 — PROHIBITED FOR COMPOUNDING**
- FDA classified BPC-157 as a "Substance with Safety Concerns" — prohibited from being compounded by licensed pharmacies (both 503A and 503B)
- "Research use only" sales (not compounding) exist in a regulatory grey area
- High enforcement priority: multiple vendor warning letters reference BPC-157 specifically
- WADA prohibited substance (S0 category) — disclaim suitability for competitive athletes

**Sermorelin: Category 1 — COMPOUNDABLE (with prescription)**
- Historically used in FDA-approved products (Geref); compoundable by 503A pharmacies with Rx
- Research use sales exist but carry moderate risk; lower enforcement priority than BPC-157

**CJC-1295 / Ipamorelin: Under Review**
- FDA PCAC (December 2024) raised concerns about safety data, characterisation, and immunogenicity risks
- Not yet prohibited for compounding; outcome of review pending
- Research chemical sales in grey area — monitor FDA PCAC updates closely

**AOD-9604: Research Chemical**
- Not FDA-approved; not on prohibited compounding list
- Australia TGA-approved for obesity — provides some legitimacy narrative
- Lower enforcement risk; growing US search interest (9x growth in 12 months)

**GHK-Cu: Relatively Lower Risk**
- Not FDA-controlled; not on compounding prohibited list
- FDA late 2023 guidance noted safety concerns but enforcement focus has been on higher-risk compounds
- Lower regulatory attention; strong female audience demographic

**Semaglutide/Tirzepatide: FDA-Approved Drugs Only**
- Compounding permitted during shortage (2024); FDA restricted this as shortage resolved (late 2024/2025)
- Cannot be sold as research chemicals under any defensible framework — do not include

**HGH / Somatropin: Controlled Substance**
- Prescription-only; Schedule III-adjacent controlled status
- Never sell as research chemical under any circumstances

**PT-141 (Bremelanotide): FDA-Approved Drug**
- Approved as Vyleesi for HSDD; selling as research chemical is high risk
- FDA can and will classify this as an unapproved new drug — defer for US launch

**Thymosin Alpha-1: Elevated Risk**
- FDA late 2023 guidance flagged "significant safety risks" and insufficient clinical data
- FDA PCAC review ongoing; not yet prohibited but elevated enforcement attention
- Legal review required before including in US product range

### FDA Enforcement Actions (2024–2026)

- **Late 2023:** FDA updated bulk drug substance lists; flagged Thymosin Alpha-1, GHK-Cu, and others as raising "significant safety risks" — from potential immune reactions to impurities and insufficient clinical data
- **Late 2024:** Warning Letters issued to entities marketing peptides online for human use. FDA explicitly stated "research use only" labels don't provide cover when products are clearly marketed for human administration
- **Warning Letters alleged:** Violations of FDCA — marketing/selling "misbranded and unapproved new drugs"
- **Escalation threatened:** Seizures or injunctions if violations not corrected within response period
- **September 2025:** FDA issued **50+ warning letters in a single month** — significant acceleration of enforcement
- **State enforcement:** Ohio Board of Pharmacy issued suspension orders and settlements; other state boards monitoring closely
- **FDA target expansion:** Enforcement increasingly targeting research-use-only peptide vendors, not just compounding pharmacies

### DEA Scheduling

Most consumer peptides are NOT DEA-controlled substances:
- BPC-157, TB-500, GHK-Cu, Sermorelin, CJC-1295, Ipamorelin: **NOT scheduled**
- HGH/Somatropin: Schedule III-adjacent; prescription-only
- Selank: CNS activity; DEA scheduling review possible — legal review required

### State-by-State Regulatory Variations

Regulatory enforcement is not uniform across US states:
- **Ohio:** Board of Pharmacy actively issuing suspension orders for peptide sales; high-risk state
- **Florida:** More permissive environment; many major US peptide vendors are Florida-based (Limitless Biotech)
- **California:** State-level enforcement tends to track or exceed FDA; elevated risk
- **Texas:** Generally permissive; large biohacking community; major longevity market
- **New York:** Consumer protection laws add complexity; tighter labelling requirements

**Strategy implication:** Consider business entity registration in a permissive state (Florida, Texas, Nevada) for reduced state-level compliance risk. Consult with state-level pharmaceutical counsel.

### US Path to Market

**Option 1: Research Chemical Model (Launch Strategy)**
- Products sold as "research chemicals" for laboratory/scientific use only
- "Research Use Only" and "Not For Human Consumption" on all products and marketing
- FDA has explicitly challenged this framing — requires meticulous compliance to minimise risk
- **No health claims, no human-use implications, no testimonials showing human administration**
- Specialist regulatory counsel required (Frier Levitt, DJ Holt Law — specialists in peptide compounding)

**Option 2: Partnership with US Compounding Pharmacy (Phase 2+)**
- Compounding pharmacies can compound non-prohibited peptides (Sermorelin, pending CJC/Ipamorelin review)
- Requires telehealth physician network; patient prescription required for each compound
- Cannot include BPC-157 in compounded preparations
- Higher compliance; much higher operational burden; potential higher margin

**Option 3: Full Telehealth Model (Phase 3)**
- Partner with or build telehealth physician network (Hims/Ro model)
- Prescriptions issued after physician consultation
- Highest compliance; highest operational complexity; highest margin potential
- Limits product range to FDA-approvable or 503A-compoundable substances

**Recommended launch approach:** Option 1 with rigorous compliance posture. Engage specialist counsel before any sales. Document all compliance decisions.

### US Regulatory Political Environment

- **RFK Jr. / MAHA Movement:** As Secretary of HHS, RFK Jr. has expressed intent to reduce FDA's aggressive peptide enforcement posture. This is a potential regulatory tailwind — but not yet enacted. Monitor policy developments in 2026.
- **FDA Budget pressures:** Significant DOGE-era FDA staff cuts in 2025 may reduce enforcement bandwidth — tactical opportunity but do not rely on this.
- **PCAC process:** FDA's Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee continues to review peptides; outcomes could open or close compounding pathways

## UK Regulatory Landscape (Phase 2 Reference)

> Relevant for Phase 2 international expansion. Comparatively more permissive than US.

### Primary Legislation

**1. The Misuse of Drugs Act 1971**
- Schedules controlled substances explicitly
- **BPC-157, TB-500, GHK-Cu, Sermorelin, CJC-1295, Ipamorelin, AOD-9604 are NOT listed** — not controlled under this act
- **HGH (Somatropin):** Explicitly controlled — do not sell

**2. Psychoactive Substances Act 2016**
- Bans production/supply of psychoactive substances
- Most peptides are NOT psychoactive (don't meaningfully cross blood-brain barrier)
- **Risk compounds:** Selank (anxiolytic), PT-141 may have CNS activity — legal review required before UK launch

**3. The Human Medicines Regulations 2012**
- KEY regulation: Makes it illegal to sell compounds for **human consumption** without MHRA approval
- Research chemical pathway available if products genuinely sold for research use only
- MHRA can classify based on intended use — marketing implying human use triggers scope

### MHRA Enforcement Posture

- Historically less aggressive than FDA
- Has taken enforcement action against SARMs marketed for human use
- Peptide-specific enforcement limited to date but a growing risk as market expands
- UK market remains comparatively permissive — Phase 2 opportunity after US brand is established

## Key Risk Areas to Avoid (US & UK)

### High Risk
1. **Explicitly marketing for human use** — Makes you a medicinal product seller in both jurisdictions
2. **Health claims** — "Heals tendons", "treats gut inflammation" — medicinal claims trigger FDCA/MHRA enforcement
3. **Selling semaglutide/tirzepatide as research chemical** — FDA/MHRA-approved prescription drugs; extremely high risk
4. **Selling HGH** — Controlled substance; do not sell
5. **Marketing to clearly non-researcher audiences** (e.g., "for athletes", "for bodybuilders") — implies human use; FDA specifically targets this

### Medium Risk
6. **Social media content showing humans self-injecting** — Implies human use; attracted FDA warning letters
7. **Testimonials claiming health outcomes** — Medicinal claims
8. **Dosage instructions as "here's how much to take"** — Crosses into prescribing territory
9. **PT-141 (US)** — FDA-approved drug; research chemical framing very high risk

### Lower Risk (with appropriate framing)
10. **Educational content on mechanisms of action** — Fine if framed as research information
11. **COA publication** — Good practice, low risk
12. **"Research use only" products** — Compliant model if genuinely maintained throughout all touchpoints
