# Longevity Peptides

> **Category file:** Epitalon · Humanin · SS-31 · MOTS-c · Thymalin · Tymogen
>
> These peptides are united by their mechanistic focus on cellular ageing, mitochondrial resilience, immune senescence, and lifespan extension. Evidence quality spans early human data (Epitalon, Thymalin), animal/in-vitro predominant (Humanin, MOTS-c, SS-31), and early bioregulator research (Tymogen).

---

## Epitalon

**Category:** Longevity / Telomere biology
**Also known as:** Epithalon, Epitalone, AEDG tetrapeptide (Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly)

### Overview & Mechanism

Epitalon is a synthetic tetrapeptide (four amino acids: Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly) originally derived from the pineal gland extract Epithalamin, first identified and developed by Dr Vladimir Khavinson at the St Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology. In 2017 researchers confirmed the peptide is present endogenously in native pineal tissue, validating it as a true bioregulator rather than a purely synthetic compound.

Its primary mechanism is **telomerase activation**: in human cell cultures, Epitalon was shown to upregulate telomerase enzyme activity, enabling cells to extend telomeres and continue dividing beyond the Hayflick limit (the normal 50–70 division ceiling). Cells treated with Epitalon underwent at least 10 additional divisions compared to controls while retaining normal morphology. Secondary mechanisms include epigenetic regulation (Epitalon binds to DNA promoter regions, loosening chromatin and potentially restoring youthful gene expression patterns), stimulation of melatonin secretion from the pineal gland (though this effect is non-linear and dose/timing-sensitive), antioxidant effects via reduction of lipid peroxidation, and modulation of circadian rhythm. Animal studies in rats and mice demonstrated life extension (up to ~40% in some protocols), reduced tumour incidence, and restored reproductive function in aged females. Evidence from Russian clinical programs in elderly patients reported improved immune markers, reduced cardiovascular morbidity, and decreased mortality over 6–15 year follow-up periods.

### Primary Use Cases

- **Longevity / anti-ageing:** Telomere maintenance and slowing of replicative senescence
- **Circadian rhythm & sleep:** Restoration of age-related decline in melatonin output
- **Immune support:** Improved immune biomarkers in elderly populations
- **Oxidative stress reduction:** Decreasing age-associated lipid peroxidation markers
- **Potential cancer protection:** Animal data show reduced tumour incidence (but also theoretical telomerase-related cancer concern — see risks)
- **Biohacker longevity stack:** Often paired with Thymalin, BPC-157, or other bioregulators in Russian-style "peptide cycling" protocols

### Evidence Quality

**Moderate (pre-clinical + early human; not robust RCTs).**

Strong mechanistic evidence at cellular level (telomerase activation confirmed in multiple in-vitro studies; PMC/PubMed indexed). Positive animal lifespan extension data in rodents and Drosophila. Human data consists primarily of Russian clinical series and observational studies led by Khavinson's institute; these have methodological limitations (lack of independent replication, non-blinded, CIS-only sample populations). A 2025 PMC study confirmed telomere elongation in human cell lines. No large-scale Western Phase II/III RCT in healthy humans. FDA classifies Epitalon as a Category 2 compound (significant safety concerns for compounding due to immunogenicity and aggregation risk — see risks). Evidence strength: **Promising but not proven by Western gold-standard trials.**

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ Concerned about **biological ageing, telomere length, or cellular senescence** as a measurable longevity target
- ✅ Experiencing **poor sleep quality or disrupted circadian rhythms**, especially in adults over 45
- ✅ Interested in a **cyclical, low-dose longevity protocol** rather than continuous supplementation
- ✅ Goals include **long-term health span extension** rather than acute performance gains
- ✅ Has existing interest in or exposure to **bioregulator peptide stacks** (Thymalin, Cortexin, etc.)
- ✅ Concerned about **age-related hormonal decline**, particularly melatonin and pineal function

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

- **Immunogenicity:** FDA (September 2023) flagged Epitalon as a Category 2 compound — compounded injectable formulations may risk immunogenic responses due to peptide aggregation and impurities
- **Theoretical cancer risk:** Telomerase activation is a double-edged sword; unchecked telomerase upregulation is a hallmark of many cancers. Animal studies show reduced tumour rates, but the long-term oncological safety in humans has not been established
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** Contraindicated — no safety data
- **Dose-dependent melatonin suppression:** At certain concentrations Epitalon may paradoxically suppress melatonin rather than stimulate it
- **No FDA approval:** Not approved as a drug in the US, UK, or EU. Compounding restrictions apply in the US. Status in Russia: established pharmaceutical use
- **WADA:** Not currently on the Prohibited List, but falls under general peptide hormone scrutiny
- **Source quality:** Significant purity variation across unregulated peptide suppliers is a real safety concern

### Education Hook Facts

1. **Epitalon allowed human cells to divide at least 10 times beyond their normal biological limit in laboratory studies** — it effectively overrode the Hayflick limit by reactivating telomerase.
2. **A 15-year Russian clinical follow-up of elderly patients taking a Thymalin/Epitalon combination reported a 1.6–4.1× reduction in mortality** compared to controls — though the study design has not been independently replicated.
3. **Epitalon was only confirmed to exist naturally in the human body in 2017**, 20+ years after it was first synthesised — validating its role as an endogenous ageing regulator.

### Dosing Reference

**Standard research/clinical protocol (from Khavinson studies and biohacker community):**
- **Dose:** 5–10 mg per day
- **Route:** Subcutaneous injection (preferred) or intranasal (lower bioavailability)
- **Cycle duration:** 10–20 consecutive days
- **Frequency:** Typically 2–4 cycles per year
- **Rest period:** 3–6 months between cycles

*Note: No FDA-approved dosing protocol exists. The above is derived from published Russian clinical studies and community protocols. Oral Epitalon has very poor bioavailability and is not considered effective by the research community.*

## Humanin

**Category:** Longevity / Mitochondrial-derived peptide
**Also known as:** HN, Gly14-Humanin (G14), HNG (potent analogue)

### Overview & Mechanism

Humanin is a 24-amino-acid peptide encoded not by nuclear DNA but by the mitochondrial genome — specifically from a small open reading frame within the 16S rRNA gene. This makes it one of the first identified **mitochondrial-derived peptides (MDPs)**, a class of signalling molecules that emerge from mitochondria in response to cellular stress, acting as inter-organelle and endocrine-like messengers. It was first discovered in 2001 by Japanese researchers investigating resistance to Alzheimer's-associated neuronal death, when they identified it as a survival factor protecting neurons from Aβ toxicity.

Humanin exerts its cytoprotective effects via multiple pathways: (1) binding to and blocking the pro-apoptotic protein BAX to prevent mitochondria-triggered cell death; (2) activating JAK2/STAT3 and PI3K/Akt pro-survival signalling; (3) acting as a ligand for the CNTFR/gp130/WSX-1 receptor complex on cell surfaces; (4) improving insulin sensitivity by reducing hepatic glucose output. Circulating humanin levels decline with age, and higher levels have been found in centenarian offspring compared to age-matched controls (Yen et al., Cell Metabolism), suggesting it may be an endogenous longevity biomarker. Exercise transiently increases circulating humanin. It also shows neuroprotective benefit against Alzheimer's, cardiovascular protection during ischaemia-reperfusion injury, and protection of the eye in macular degeneration models.

### Primary Use Cases

- **Neuroprotection:** Defence against Alzheimer's pathology, neuronal apoptosis
- **Metabolic health:** Improved insulin sensitivity, protection from metabolic syndrome
- **Longevity / anti-ageing:** Restoration of age-declining MDP signalling; part of mitochondrial health stacks
- **Cardiovascular protection:** Reduced ischaemic injury in animal models
- **Eye health:** Protection against age-related macular degeneration (AMD) in animal models
- **Biohacker stack:** Often paired with MOTS-c and SS-31 as a "mitochondrial triad"

### Evidence Quality

**Preclinical + limited human observational. No clinical RCTs in humans.**

Mechanistic evidence is robust in cell lines and animal models (mice, rodents). Human observational data show an association between higher circulating humanin and longevity phenotypes (centenarian studies). The Alzheimer's Disease Research Center (ADRC) rates it as showing "promising neuroprotective benefit" but notes "unknown utility as a therapeutic in humans" and that "clinical human testing has not been conducted." Therapeutic dosing in humans has not been established. Evidence strength: **Promising mechanistic foundation; clinical proof-of-concept lacking.**

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ Concerned about **cognitive decline, memory, or Alzheimer's prevention** as a longevity priority
- ✅ Has a **family history of Alzheimer's disease** or early cognitive changes themselves
- ✅ Interested in **mitochondrial health optimisation** as a core longevity intervention
- ✅ Experiencing **metabolic dysfunction** — insulin resistance, blood glucose dysregulation
- ✅ Interested in **stacking with MOTS-c or SS-31** as part of a mitochondrial peptide protocol
- ✅ Seeks peptides with a **theoretical longevity biomarker link** (centenarian phenotype association)

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

- **No clinical safety data in humans:** Therapeutic dosing protocols do not exist from human trials; all safety inferences are extrapolated from animal studies
- **Sex-dependent effects:** Some evidence suggests humanin's effects may differ between males and females (ADRC notes)
- **Context-dependent activity:** In some contexts, humanin may promote survival of cells you don't want to survive (e.g., cancer cells resistant to apoptosis) — a theoretical concern requiring more research
- **Short half-life:** ~2–4 hours, requiring frequent dosing in research settings
- **Not FDA approved, not scheduled:** Research compound status; legal to possess in most jurisdictions but not approved for human therapeutic use
- **WADA:** Not listed; not a monitored substance
- **No compounding pharmacy sourcing:** Extremely limited availability through regulated channels

### Education Hook Facts

1. **Humanin is encoded by mitochondrial DNA, not the cell nucleus** — it was the first clue that mitochondria send their own peptide signals to regulate the rest of the cell's fate.
2. **Centenarians' adult children have measurably higher circulating humanin levels** than age-matched controls — suggesting it may be a heritable longevity signal.
3. **Exercise increases circulating humanin levels** — one more reason physical activity may extend healthspan at a molecular level.

### Dosing Reference

**No established human dosing protocol exists.**

Animal research reference doses (for context only):
- **Mice:** 4 mg/kg subcutaneous injection
- **Community exploration doses:** Anecdotal reports of 0.5–1 mg subcutaneous injection, 2–3× per week
- Short half-life (2–4 hours) necessitates frequent dosing in experimental settings

*HNG (Gly14-Humanin) is a synthetic analogue with ~1000× greater potency in some assays but with even less human safety data. Approach with significant caution.*

## SS-31

**Category:** Longevity / Mitochondrial-targeted peptide
**Also known as:** Elamipretide, MTP-131, Bendavia, D-Arg-2′6′-Dmt-Lys-Phe-NH2

### Overview & Mechanism

SS-31 (Szeto-Schiller peptide 31) is an aromatic-cationic tetrapeptide that selectively targets the inner mitochondrial membrane by binding to **cardiolipin**, a phospholipid uniquely concentrated there and critical for maintaining the structure of the electron transport chain (ETC). Unlike most antioxidants, SS-31 is not consumed in the process — it acts catalytically to stabilise cardiolipin and prevent its oxidation by cytochrome c. By doing so, it preserves ETC function, enhances ATP production, and reduces pathological reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation.

This mechanism makes SS-31 particularly relevant to diseases of mitochondrial dysfunction: ageing (where cardiolipin composition and quantity decline), heart failure, ischaemia-reperfusion injury, Barth syndrome (a genetic cardiolipin disorder), and sarcopenia. In September 2025, the FDA granted accelerated approval for elamipretide (the pharmaceutical-grade version) for the treatment of **Barth syndrome**, making it the first drug approved to directly target mitochondria. In Barth syndrome clinical trials (TAZPOWER), 8 patients showed an average 96.1 metre improvement in 6-minute walk distance over 168 weeks, with improved cardiac stroke volume and cardiolipin levels. Clinical trials in heart failure (HFpEF) have been conducted but results are mixed — the Phase II PROGRESS-HF trial showed improved exercise capacity in some subgroups.

### Primary Use Cases

- **Ageing / mitochondrial decline:** Restoring age-related loss of cardiolipin integrity and ETC efficiency
- **Cardiac protection:** Heart failure, ischaemia-reperfusion injury, post-MI recovery
- **Exercise performance & recovery:** Improved mitochondrial ATP production for endurance athletes
- **Sarcopenia:** Preserving skeletal muscle mitochondrial function in ageing
- **Rare mitochondrial diseases:** Barth syndrome (FDA approved, 2025), MELAS, and similar disorders
- **Biohacker "mitochondrial triad":** Often stacked with MOTS-c and Humanin

### Evidence Quality

**Strongest evidence among the longevity peptides in this file — FDA accelerated approval achieved for Barth syndrome (2025).**

Animal model evidence is robust. Human clinical trials conducted in heart failure (Phase II) with mixed primary endpoint results. TAZPOWER Phase 2/3 trial in Barth syndrome showed functional improvement sufficient for FDA accelerated approval. Longer-term open-label extension data (168 weeks) published in Genetics in Medicine (2024). The main caveat: Barth syndrome approval does not extrapolate to anti-ageing use in healthy people. Evidence for ageing-specific use remains preclinical/mechanistic. Evidence strength: **Strong for mitochondrial disease; preclinical for anti-ageing.**

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ Concerned about **heart health, cardiac efficiency, or post-cardiac event recovery**
- ✅ Experiencing **exercise intolerance, fatigue, or declining aerobic capacity** not explained by other causes
- ✅ Interested in a peptide with **actual FDA approval history** (Barth syndrome), giving some regulatory validation of the mechanism
- ✅ Has a **family history of mitochondrial disease** or confirmed mitochondrial dysfunction markers
- ✅ Focused on **muscle preservation and energy metabolism** as primary longevity goals
- ✅ Has **systolic or diastolic heart failure** or is at cardiovascular risk — with physician oversight

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

- **Not approved for anti-ageing use:** The FDA accelerated approval is specifically and only for Barth syndrome; off-label use has no regulatory backing
- **Mixed heart failure trial results:** Phase II HFpEF trial did not consistently hit primary endpoints across all analyses
- **Injection site reactions:** Most common adverse effect in trials
- **Limited long-term safety data in healthy individuals:** Safety profile extrapolated from disease populations
- **WADA:** Not currently listed as a prohibited substance
- **Regulatory:** Research compound in most countries outside of Barth syndrome indication. Compounding availability limited
- **Cost:** Pharmaceutical elamipretide is extremely expensive; research-grade SS-31 quality varies significantly

### Education Hook Facts

1. **SS-31 is the first drug ever approved by the FDA that directly targets mitochondria** — its 2025 approval for Barth syndrome validated a decades-old hypothesis that cardiolipin is a druggable target.
2. **SS-31 acts without being consumed** — it works catalytically at the inner mitochondrial membrane, which is why tiny doses can have outsized effects compared to conventional antioxidants.
3. **Cardiolipin — the molecule SS-31 targets — declines with normal ageing in heart and skeletal muscle**, which is one reason energy production deteriorates as we get older.

### Dosing Reference

**Barth syndrome clinical trial protocol (for reference):**
- **Dose:** 40 mg/day subcutaneous infusion (continuous) or 40 mg subcutaneous injection once daily
- **Duration:** Long-term (168-week open-label extension)

**Biohacker/research community protocol:**
- **Dose:** 5–10 mg subcutaneous injection, 3–5× per week
- **Cycle:** 4–8 weeks on, with rest periods

*Note: No established dosing in healthy humans exists. The above biohacker protocol is anecdotal and not validated in clinical studies.*

## MOTS-c

**Category:** Longevity / Mitochondrial-derived peptide / Metabolic
**Also known as:** Mitochondrial Open reading frame of the Twelve S rRNA-c, MDPs, M-peptide

### Overview & Mechanism

MOTS-c (Mitochondrial Open reading frame of the 12S rRNA-c) is a 16-amino-acid peptide encoded within mitochondrial DNA, discovered in 2015 by the Yen lab at USC. Like Humanin, it belongs to the **mitochondrial-derived peptide (MDP)** class. Its primary mechanism is **AMPK activation**: MOTS-c enters the cell nucleus and inhibits the folate cycle, leading to accumulation of AICAR (an AMP analog), which activates AMPK — a master metabolic sensor. Activated AMPK drives cells toward energy-efficient mode: enhanced glucose uptake, increased fatty acid oxidation, improved mitochondrial biogenesis, and reduced fat storage.

Endogenous MOTS-c levels increase ~11.9-fold in skeletal muscle during exercise (with levels remaining elevated for 4 hours post-exercise), identifying it as a key exercise mimetic signal. Importantly, MOTS-c levels **decline with age**, correlating with the metabolic deterioration seen in older adults. Animal studies show MOTS-c administration improves insulin sensitivity, prevents diet-induced obesity, enhances physical performance, extends lifespan in aged mice, and promotes thermogenesis in subcutaneous white adipose tissue. A pilot study in humans demonstrated pharmacokinetic feasibility, but large-scale human efficacy trials have not been completed.

### Primary Use Cases

- **Metabolic health:** Improving insulin sensitivity, glucose regulation, weight management
- **Exercise performance:** Boosting mitochondrial ATP production and exercise capacity
- **Anti-ageing:** Restoring age-declining metabolic flexibility and mitochondrial function
- **Obesity prevention:** Anti-obesity effects through AMPK and thermogenesis pathways
- **Longevity stacking:** Often used alongside Humanin and SS-31 as part of a mitochondrial-focused protocol
- **Muscle preservation:** Supporting anabolic signalling in ageing muscle via AMPK/insulin sensitivity

### Evidence Quality

**Preclinical (strong) + very limited human pilot data.**

Strong mechanistic and animal model evidence: insulin sensitivity improvements, obesity prevention, and lifespan extension in rodents are well-documented (multiple PMC-indexed studies). Human data is limited to pharmacokinetic pilot studies and observational data (circulating MOTS-c levels in athletes vs sedentary populations). No Phase II/III RCTs in humans. Evidence strength: **Mechanistically compelling; human clinical evidence at very early stage.**

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ Experiencing **metabolic dysfunction** — insulin resistance, elevated fasting glucose, difficulty losing fat despite diet and exercise
- ✅ Goals include **mimicking the metabolic benefits of exercise** even when exercise capacity is limited
- ✅ Concerned about **age-related metabolic decline** — the "metabolic ageing" phenomenon
- ✅ Interested in peptides with a **mitochondrial biology basis** and an exercise-science connection
- ✅ Has been told they have **pre-diabetes or metabolic syndrome**
- ✅ Wants to **preserve metabolic health and body composition** as a core longevity strategy

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

- **No established human safety data from trials:** Safety extrapolated from animal models and limited human pharmacokinetics
- **AMPK activation concerns:** Theoretical risk of excessive AMPK signalling suppressing mTOR-dependent anabolism; stacking with high-protein/resistance training protocols may be needed to prevent muscle loss
- **FDA Category 2:** Listed alongside Epitalon, AOD-9604, and others as a compound with significant safety concerns for compounding (immunogenicity, insufficient clinical evidence)
- **Not FDA approved for any indication**
- **WADA:** Not on the Prohibited List
- **Short half-life:** Requires frequent dosing to maintain circulating levels
- **Purity concerns:** Research-grade suppliers vary widely in peptide purity

### Education Hook Facts

1. **MOTS-c is produced by mitochondrial DNA, not the cell's nucleus** — one of very few peptides encoded outside the nuclear genome, suggesting it evolved specifically as a stress-response signal.
2. **A single bout of exercise increases skeletal muscle MOTS-c by nearly 12-fold** — making it the molecular link between exercise and metabolic health that scientists have been searching for.
3. **Older adults have measurably lower MOTS-c levels than younger people**, even when matched for body weight — suggesting MOTS-c decline is a direct contributor to age-related metabolic deterioration.

### Dosing Reference

**Research/pilot human protocols:**
- **Dose:** 5–10 mg subcutaneous injection
- **Frequency:** 3–5× per week
- **Cycle:** 4–8 weeks on, with washout periods
- **Timing:** Often timed around exercise sessions given its exercise-mimetic role

*Animal studies typically used 5–15 mg/kg/day. Human equivalent doses are uncertain; the community protocol above is extrapolated from pharmacokinetic pilot data and biohacker experience, not clinical trial data.*

## Thymalin

**Category:** Longevity / Immunomodulation / Thymic bioregulator
**Also known as:** Thymus polypeptide complex, Tactivin (similar product), Thymus extract

### Overview & Mechanism

Thymalin is a polypeptide complex extracted from the thymus glands of young calves, containing a mixture of short peptides (2–8 amino acids) that regulate immune cell differentiation, haematopoiesis, and T-cell maturation. It was developed by Dr Vladimir Khavinson's team at the St Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology and has been an approved pharmaceutical in Russia since the 1970s for conditions involving immune dysfunction. Unlike synthetic single-peptide bioregulators (like Epitalon or Tymogen), Thymalin is a complex natural extract with multiple active peptide components.

Its mechanism centres on the **thymus-immune axis**: the thymus involutes (shrinks) with age from roughly 40–50 years, dramatically reducing the output of naïve T-cells and degrading adaptive immunity. Thymalin is hypothesised to partially restore thymic function by supplying short peptides that bind DNA promoter regions in immune and haematopoietic cells, promoting transcription of genes involved in T-cell maturation and differentiation. In a landmark 15-year geroprotective study (Khavinson & Morozov, Neuroendocrinology Letters, 2003), elderly patients given Thymalin + Epithalamin twice per year showed a 2-fold reduction in all-cause mortality compared to controls. A 2022 Italian study (Avolio et al., IJMS) found Thymalin reduced inflammatory cytokine production in human THP-1 macrophage cells — the first non-Russian mechanistic validation.

### Primary Use Cases

- **Immune restoration in ageing:** Rebuilding adaptive immunity lost to thymic involution
- **Post-infection or post-treatment immune recovery:** Used in Russian medicine after cancer treatment, severe infection, or surgery
- **Anti-ageing longevity protocols:** Part of Russian bioregulator stacks alongside Epitalon
- **Chronic infection susceptibility:** Repeated viral or bacterial infections in older adults
- **Haematopoietic support:** Potentially useful in blood cell production disorders
- **COVID-19 recovery (research):** A 2021 Russian RCT (Advances in Gerontology) explored Thymalin in COVID patients

### Evidence Quality

**Moderate — substantial clinical use in Russia/CIS; independent Western validation very limited.**

Decades of Russian clinical use and Khavinson-group studies showing reduced mortality and improved immune markers in elderly populations. The Avolio et al. 2022 Italian study (in THP-1 cells) provided partial independent mechanistic validation. The broader thymic peptide class has solid mechanistic backing (reviewed in Blood Neoplasia, ASH 2025). However, no truly independent, large-scale Western RCT has been published. Evidence strength: **Mechanistically plausible, extensive clinical use in CIS; needs independent replication.**

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ Experiencing **frequent infections, slow recovery, or declining immune function** — especially in adults over 50
- ✅ **Post-chemotherapy or immunocompromised** status with desire to restore immune competence
- ✅ Interested in **bioregulator peptide protocols** and Russian longevity medicine approaches
- ✅ Concerned about **immunosenescence** — the progressive deterioration of immune response with age
- ✅ Has chronic conditions linked to **immune dysregulation** (autoimmune, chronic low-grade infections)
- ✅ Seeking a peptide with **established clinical precedent** (even if primarily from Russian medicine)

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

- **Animal-derived:** Risk of prion contamination or pathogen transmission from bovine source material — quality of sourcing is critical; pharmaceutical-grade is much safer than unregulated peptide suppliers
- **Immunostimulation risk:** Could theoretically worsen autoimmune conditions by stimulating already-overactive immune responses
- **Not FDA approved:** Research compound outside Russia/CIS; no Western regulatory approval
- **WADA:** Not listed as prohibited
- **Allergenicity:** Complex polypeptide extract may trigger allergic responses in sensitive individuals
- **Standardisation issues:** As a natural extract, potency can vary between batches; synthetic Khavinson peptides (Tymogen, Vilon) offer more consistency

### Education Hook Facts

1. **Your thymus — the immune system's training school — shrinks to almost nothing by age 50**, reducing the output of fresh T-cells and leaving the immune system running on an ageing repertoire. Thymalin was designed to partially reverse this.
2. **In a 15-year Russian clinical study, elderly patients taking Thymalin and Epithalamin twice per year had approximately half the mortality rate** of age-matched controls — a striking (though not independently replicated) finding.
3. **Thymalin has been an approved pharmaceutical in Russia since the 1970s** — making it one of the oldest peptide therapeutics still in active clinical use.

### Dosing Reference

**Russian clinical protocol:**
- **Dose:** 5–20 mg intramuscular injection (pharmaceutical-grade Thymalin)
- **Frequency:** Daily for 3–10 days, or every other day
- **Cycle:** 1–2 courses per year; some protocols use twice-yearly administration
- **Route:** Intramuscular injection (most studied); subcutaneous also used in research

*Note: Dosing is from published Russian clinical studies. No FDA-approved protocol exists. Quality and potency of research-grade Thymalin varies enormously between suppliers.*

## Tymogen

**Category:** Longevity / Immunomodulation / Synthetic thymic dipeptide
**Also known as:** Thymogen, Glu-Trp dipeptide, Thymagen, EW dipeptide (Glutamic acid-Tryptophan)

### Overview & Mechanism

Tymogen (also spelled Thymogen) is a synthetic dipeptide composed of just two amino acids: **Glutamic acid (Glu) and Tryptophan (Trp)**, written as Glu-Trp. It was developed by Khavinson's team as the minimal bioactive unit isolated from Thymalin — the idea being that the immunostimulatory effect of the complex extract could be replicated with a simple, consistently manufacturable synthetic molecule.

Its mechanism involves modulation of cyclic nucleotide signalling in T-cells: Tymogen appears to influence the balance between cAMP and cGMP (through phosphodiesterase modulation), which regulates T-cell activation thresholds and differentiation. At the gene expression level, it is hypothesised to interact with DNA promoter regions containing specific motifs to promote transcription of T-cell maturation genes, consistent with the broader Khavinson "peptide bioregulation" model. Research also shows Tymogen modulates TLR/RLR-dependent innate immune signalling pathways (Sokolova et al., referenced in immunostimulation studies), offering an additional mechanism through pattern recognition receptor pathways. In a 2022 Italian study (Avolio et al., IJMS) Tymogen was tested in THP-1 macrophage cells alongside Epitalon, Thymalin, and other bioregulators, showing reduced inflammatory cytokine production — the best independent mechanistic study to date.

### Primary Use Cases

- **Immune support in ageing:** T-cell population restoration, improved adaptive immunity
- **T-cell differentiation support:** Specifically targeting the CD4/CD8 axis and regulatory T-cell balance
- **Companion to Thymalin protocols:** Used when a consistent, synthetic alternative to the extract is preferred
- **Infection susceptibility:** Chronic or recurrent viral infections in immunosenescent adults
- **Post-illness immune recovery:** Restoring immune competence following significant illness or stress

### Evidence Quality

**Low–moderate. Primarily Khavinson-group studies with partial independent validation.**

The core mechanistic concept (thymic dipeptide immunostimulation) is scientifically plausible and backed by Khavinson's three decades of research. The Avolio et al. 2022 study in human macrophage cells provides partial independent validation. Morozov & Khavinson's 1997 ScienceDirect review reached international audiences. However, no dedicated independent clinical RCT exists outside Russia/CIS. Evidence strength: **Mechanistically plausible; evidence base narrow and primarily from one research group.**

### Quiz Signals

- ✅ Same profile as Thymalin users but preferring a **consistent, synthetic compound** over a complex animal extract
- ✅ Looking for a **shorter, simpler peptide** with a cleaner pharmacological profile for immune support
- ✅ Experiencing **declining immune function or increased susceptibility to illness** with age
- ✅ Interested in the **Khavinson bioregulator system** as part of a broader longevity protocol
- ✅ Prefers **lower risk of allergic reaction** compared to complex polypeptide thymus extracts
- ✅ Seeking **cost-effective immune optimisation** — as a dipeptide, Tymogen is simpler and cheaper to synthesise than complex extracts

### Risks & Regulatory Notes

- **Limited independent safety data:** Most safety evidence from Russian clinical practice; no published independent Phase I safety trial
- **Immunostimulation risk:** As with Thymalin — potential for worsening autoimmune conditions
- **Synthetic dipeptide:** Lower risk of contamination/prion issues compared to animal-derived extracts, but purity of research-grade product varies
- **Not FDA approved:** Research compound in US/UK/EU
- **Not on WADA Prohibited List**
- **Regulatory status in Russia:** Approved pharmaceutical (Tymogen injection and nasal spray)
- **Intranasal option:** A nasal spray formulation exists in Russian pharmaceutical practice, offering a non-injectable route

### Education Hook Facts

1. **Tymogen is just two amino acids long** — making it one of the smallest immunologically active peptides known, demonstrating that enormous biological complexity can emerge from minimal molecular structure.
2. **It was reverse-engineered from Thymalin** — scientists identified the two-amino-acid sequence within the complex extract responsible for its immune effects, then synthesised it alone, achieving similar results with far greater consistency.
3. **Tymogen has been available as a licensed nasal spray in Russia since the 1980s** — one of the longest-established synthetic peptide pharmaceuticals in clinical use.

### Dosing Reference

**Russian pharmaceutical and clinical research protocol:**
- **Injectable (IM or SC):** 50–100 mcg (0.05–0.1 mg) per day for 3–10 days
- **Nasal spray (Russian licensed form):** 1–2 sprays per nostril, 2× daily for 7–10 days
- **Cycle frequency:** 1–2 courses per year or as clinically indicated

*Note: Dosing is far lower than Thymalin on a per-mg basis, reflecting its higher purity and potency as an isolated synthetic dipeptide. No FDA-validated protocol exists.*
